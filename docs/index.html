Part IOverview
01 What this is

Purpose

Hyperbolic neural networks are ordinary neural networks whose internal points live on a curved surface — the Poincaré ball — instead of in flat space. This builds one from the ground up, every equation included, ending at a working sentence-pair classifier.

The reason to bother is hierarchical data: taxonomies, ontologies, file trees, category systems. Such data is tree-shaped, and trees grow exponentially — ten children per node gives 10, then 100, then 1,000 nodes at successive depths. A network stores each node as a point and compares nodes by distance, so it must place those exponentially many nodes far enough apart to keep them distinct. Euclidean space cannot supply the room: the volume within radius $r$ grows only polynomially in $r$, while the tree needs it to grow exponentially.

That gap is not abstract; it shows up as two concrete failures. To keep exponentially many descendants apart you must either spend exponentially many coordinates per point — directly more memory and compute per node — or pack the points exponentially close together, in which case their coordinates need more precision than a 32- or 64-bit float can store, so distinct nodes round to the same number and become impossible to tell apart. Both failures show up as high distortion: the distance between two points in the embedding stops matching their true distance in the tree. A parent ends up no nearer its child than two unrelated leaves, and the network can no longer read the hierarchy at all.

Hyperbolic space closes the gap because its volume grows exponentially with radius — the tree's own rate. The tree then embeds with almost no distortion (root near the centre, each level fanning further out) in as few as two dimensions, so no precision is lost and no extra dimensions are needed. A hyperbolic neural network simply puts its representations on that surface. (Part III shows the volume gap directly.)

Part IIHistory — where the geometry comes from
03 Euclid's postulates

Five rules, and a suspect fifth

Around 300 BCE Euclid built plane geometry on five postulates — statements assumed without proof. The first four are simple. The fifth is longer and was long suspected of being a theorem in disguise.

Euclid · Elements, Book I — the five postulates

The first four alone define neutral geometry: every result they prove holds no matter what you later assume about parallels. The fifth is the branch point. For two thousand years mathematicians tried to prove it from the first four — to show it was redundant.

04 The attempts

Trying to prove the fifth

The sharpest attempts assumed the fifth was false and looked for a contradiction. None appeared; the assumption just kept producing consistent, if strange, theorems.

1733 — Saccheri
Giovanni Saccheri derives many theorems from the negation of the fifth postulate, hoping one will be absurd. He finds results he dislikes, but no contradiction.
1766 — Lambert
Johann Lambert shows that under the negation a triangle's angle defect is proportional to its area, and suggests the geometry behaves like "a sphere of imaginary radius." We will take that remark literally below.

By the early 1800s the likely explanation was that there was no contradiction to find — that the fifth postulate is independent of the other four.

05 The break

A consistent alternative

Gauss (privately), Lobachevsky (1829), and Bolyai (1832) stopped trying to prove the fifth and replaced it, then built out the resulting geometry. The replacement is simply the negation of Euclid's parallel rule:

The hyperbolic axiom the one rule that changes

Euclid's fifth postulate is equivalent to Playfair's axiom: through a point not on a line $\ell$ there is exactly one line parallel to $\ell$. The hyperbolic axiom changes "exactly one" to "at least two": through a point not on $\ell$ there are at least two distinct lines that never meet $\ell$.

It is nothing more than the negation of the parallel postulate — the assumption Saccheri and Lambert had pushed on for a century hoping to break it. Keep Euclid's first four postulates, add this one in place of the fifth, and you have hyperbolic geometry. In neutral geometry "at least two" immediately forces infinitely many.

Bolyai wrote to his father, "I have created a new, another universe out of nothing." Drag the point below: every cyan line through it misses $\ell$. Euclid allowed one such line; here there are infinitely many, bounded by two limiting parallels that approach $\ell$ without ever touching it.

06 What changes

Triangles lose angle

The new axiom has a measurable effect: a triangle's angles sum to less than $\pi$ (180°), and the shortfall grows with the triangle's size. Stating this precisely needs one idea — curvature.

Curvature, and the angle defect how much a space bends

Build a triangle from three shortest-path sides and add its interior angles. On a flat plane the sum is exactly $\pi$ (180°). On a sphere it is more than $\pi$; the surplus $(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)-\pi$ is the angle excess. On a saddle it is less; the shortfall $\pi-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)$ is the angle defect.

Curvature is that surplus or shortfall measured per unit area of the triangle: positive where angles bulge (sphere), negative where they pinch (saddle), zero when flat. Constant curvature means the same value everywhere; hyperbolic geometry is the constant-negative case. Taking "curvature = angle excess per unit area" as the working definition, the next derivation pins down the number — and along the way proves the curvature of a sphere.

The parameter $c$ the amount of curving

The number $c>0$ sets the magnitude of curvature: the hyperbolic plane has curvature $-c$. Larger $c$ means more sharply curved; smaller $c$ looks Euclidean over a larger region. Curvature is an angle defect divided by an area, so $c$ carries units of $1/\text{length}^2$ — which (derived shortly) gives the space a built-in length $1/\sqrt c$. Every figure here uses $c=1$.

The exact link between angle defect and area is a classical result. Rather than quote it, we can derive it — first on a sphere, where curvature is easy to see, then carry it across to negative curvature.

Derivation — a sphere's curvature is 1/R², and defect = c · Areacounting lunes
$$(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)-\pi=\frac{\operatorname{Area}}{R^2}\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \pi-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)=c\cdot\operatorname{Area}$$
1

Work on a sphere of radius $R$ using only its ordinary geometry — nothing about curvature assumed yet. Two great circles cross at opposite points and cut the sphere into four wedges (lunes). A lune's area is proportional to its opening angle $\theta$ (twice the angle, twice the wedge); the whole sphere ($\theta=2\pi$) has area $4\pi R^2$, so a single lune of angle $\theta$ has area $2\theta R^2$, and a lune together with its opposite has area $4\theta R^2$.

2

Take a triangle with angles $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ and extend its three sides to full great circles. The three opposite-lune pairs (angles $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$) together cover the whole sphere, except they cover the triangle and its mirror-image antipode three times instead of once. Counting total area with that multiplicity:

$$4R^2(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)=\underbrace{4\pi R^2}_{\text{whole sphere, once}}+\underbrace{4\,\operatorname{Area}}_{\text{triangle + antipode, two extra times each}}.$$
3

Divide by $4R^2$ and rearrange. The angle excess over $\pi$ equals the area divided by $R^2$ — derived purely from areas, with no curvature put in by hand:

$$(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)-\pi=\frac{\operatorname{Area}}{R^2}.$$

This is Girard's theorem (1629). Now apply the working definition from the box — curvature is angle excess per unit area. Reading it off: the sphere's curvature is $K=\dfrac{\text{excess}}{\operatorname{Area}}=\dfrac{1}{R^2}$. That is where "a sphere of radius $R$ has curvature $1/R^2$" comes from — it is not assumed, it is the count above.

∎

Carry the law to negative curvature with Lambert's hint that the hyperbolic plane is "a sphere of imaginary radius." Set $R=i\rho$, so $R^2=-\rho^2$ and the per-area constant flips sign: $1/R^2=-1/\rho^2$, which we name $-c$. Excess becomes deficit:

$$(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)-\pi=-c\,\operatorname{Area}\;\Longrightarrow\;\pi-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)=c\cdot\operatorname{Area}.$$

The imaginary-radius substitution is a heuristic bridge; the honest statement is that "excess (or deficit) = curvature × area" is the local Gauss–Bonnet law for any constant-curvature surface, proven here on the sphere.

This single formula has heavy consequences. Triangle area is bounded: no triangle exceeds $\pi/c$, the area of an "ideal" triangle with all three corners at infinity and zero angles. There are no similar triangles — fix the three angles and the size is fixed too. And the geometry has an absolute unit of length, set by $c$. Drag the corners toward the edge: the angle sum collapses while the area saturates.

07 Making it concrete

A model you can compute in

Lobachevsky and Bolyai had a consistent-looking theory, but "no contradiction found" is not "no contradiction exists." The question was settled by building a model.

Model a concrete realization of axioms

A model of a set of axioms is a concrete construction — made from objects you already trust — in which every axiom is true. It assigns the undefined words ("point," "line") to specific things and checks that the axioms hold. Build a model of hyperbolic geometry inside Euclidean geometry, and any contradiction derivable in hyperbolic geometry would translate into one in Euclidean geometry. So a model proves relative consistency: the new system is exactly as trustworthy as the old.

1868 — Beltrami
Eugenio Beltrami builds hyperbolic geometry on a real surface of constant negative curvature — the first model, and the first proof of relative consistency.
1882 — Poincaré
Henri Poincaré finds the conformal disk model: the whole infinite hyperbolic plane drawn inside an open disk, with straight lines shown as circular arcs meeting the boundary at right angles, and angles drawn at true size. This is the model the network uses.

The verdict: hyperbolic geometry is consistent exactly when Euclidean geometry is, so the fifth postulate is independent — the two-thousand-year search for a proof was chasing something that cannot exist. We adopt Poincaré's model.

The Poincaré ball

The Poincaré ball is the open disk of radius $1/\sqrt c$ — all points with $c\lVert x\rVert^2<1$, where $\lVert x\rVert$ is ordinary Euclidean length. Before going further, two things owed from earlier: where the radius $1/\sqrt c$ comes from, and what the metric actually is.

Derivation — the ball's radius is 1/√ccurvature sets a length
$$\text{radius}=\frac{1}{\sqrt c}$$
1

Units. An angle in radians is a ratio of two lengths (arc length over radius), so it is a pure number with no units — and the angle defect is just such a number. Area is a length times a length, so it carries units of $\text{length}^2$. From the last derivation, curvature is defect ÷ area = (pure number) ÷ $\text{length}^2$, so its units are $1/\text{length}^2$. Hence $c$ has units $1/\text{length}^2$, and $1/\sqrt c$ has units of length — the one length the curvature alone can build.

2

That length is the hyperbolic version of a sphere's radius. We just proved a sphere of radius $R$ has curvature $1/R^2$, i.e. $R=1/\sqrt{K}$ — the radius is one over the square root of the curvature. With curvature magnitude $c$, the matching length is $\rho=1/\sqrt c$, the space's radius of curvature (the same $\rho$ as in the imaginary-radius step).

∎

The model draws the whole infinite plane inside this finite disk, so distances must grow without bound as you approach the edge — a fixed Euclidean step has to count for ever more hyperbolic length, putting the rim infinitely far away. The metric (next) does exactly that: its stretch factor runs to infinity as $c\lVert x\rVert^2\to1$, i.e. at Euclidean radius $\lVert x\rVert=1/\sqrt c$. So the disk's radius is the radius of curvature.

With c = 1 the ball is the unit disk, radius 1 — the choice in every figure here.

The rule that stretches distances toward the rim is the metric; the amount of stretch at each point is one number, $\lambda$. Two definitions, then two facts about the space that fix $\lambda$ entirely.

Metric the ruler at each point

A metric is the rule a space uses to measure the length of a tiny step (and the angle between two steps) at each point. Formally it is an inner product $g_x$ — a dot-product-like operation — attached to the small arrows (tangent vectors) at every point $x$. The length of a path, and so the distance between two points, comes from adding up those tiny measurements along it. Flat space uses one dot product everywhere; a curved space lets $g_x$ vary from point to point.

Homogeneous same at every point

A space is homogeneous if it looks identical from every point: for any two points there is a distance-preserving motion (an isometry) carrying one to the other. No point is special; there is no true "centre." The hyperbolic plane is homogeneous — the disk picture only makes the middle look special, which is an artifact of the drawing.

Isotropic same in every direction

A space is isotropic at a point if it looks identical in every direction from there: the isometries fixing that point can rotate any direction into any other. No direction is special. The hyperbolic plane is isotropic at every point. Homogeneous and isotropic together mean the geometry has no distinguishing feature anywhere — and that total symmetry is what pins the metric down.

Conformal factor $\lambda$ "lambda" — the local stretch

The Poincaré metric is the ordinary dot product times one positive number, $\lambda_x^2$. That $\lambda_x$ is the conformal factor: how much hyperbolic length is stretched relative to Euclidean length at $x$. Conformal means the stretch is the same in every direction, so angles are preserved. Near the centre ($\lambda\approx2$) the space is almost flat; toward the rim ($\lambda\to\infty$) a tiny Euclidean step becomes an enormous hyperbolic distance — which is how an infinite space fits in a finite disk.

Now we find $\lambda$. Two facts about the space do it between them: its symmetry forces the shape of $\lambda$ (how it depends on position), and its curvature forces the single constant out in front — the mysterious 2. The symmetry step needs the disk's own family of symmetry maps, so we meet those first; the curvature step needs one standard formula, in the toolbox below.

Möbius transformation (of the disk) the disk's symmetry maps

It is far easier to work if we treat the flat plane as the complex numbers: a point $(x_1,x_2)$ is written as one number $z=x_1+ix_2$, its Euclidean length is $\lvert z\rvert$, and $\bar z$ is its mirror image (the conjugate). This is only a relabelling, but it turns the disk's symmetry maps into one-line formulas.

A Möbius transformation of the disk is a map of the form $\varphi_a(z)=\dfrac{z-a}{1-\bar a z}$, one for each interior point $a$ (optionally followed by a rotation). Two properties matter. It sends the unit disk onto itself one-to-one, and it preserves angles. And it does one concrete job: it slides the point $a$ to the centre, because $\varphi_a(a)=\frac{a-a}{1-\lvert a\rvert^2}=0$.

Why these are the isometries. Homogeneity (defined above) says: for any point there must be a distance-preserving motion — an isometry — carrying it to the centre. The metric is conformal, so any isometry also preserves angles; and it must keep the disk the disk. The maps that send the disk to itself preserving angles are exactly the $\varphi_a$ (a classical fact of complex analysis, the Schwarz lemma). So the hyperbolic isometries can only be these. We will use that the true metric cannot change under any $\varphi_a$.

⊞Toolbox · curvature of a conformal metric·

For a 2-D metric that is the flat one scaled by a function, $ds=\lambda(x)\,\lVert dx\rVert$, the Gaussian curvature is

$$K=-\frac{1}{\lambda^2}\,\Delta\big(\ln\lambda\big),\qquad \Delta=\partial_{x_1}^2+\partial_{x_2}^2.$$

This is the standard curvature formula in conformal coordinates ($\Delta$ is the ordinary Laplacian — the sum of unmixed second derivatives). Deriving it needs Christoffel symbols, so we take it as given and use it to pin down $\lambda$.

Derivation — the conformal factor, and why the 2symmetry fixes the shape, curvature the scale
$$\lambda_x=\frac{2}{1-c\lVert x\rVert^2}$$
1

What we are solving for. "Conformal" means the metric is the flat ruler scaled at each point by an unknown positive function: a tiny step of Euclidean length $\lvert dz\rvert$ has hyperbolic length $ds=\lambda(z)\,\lvert dz\rvert$. The whole task is to find that one function $\lambda(z)$. We work in the unit disk ($c=1$) for clean formulas and restore $c$ at the end.

2

The condition $\lambda$ must satisfy. Each map $\varphi_a$ is an isometry, so it cannot change the length of any step. If $w=\varphi_a(z)$, a step transforms by the derivative, $\lvert dw\rvert=\lvert\varphi_a'(z)\rvert\,\lvert dz\rvert$. Demanding equal hyperbolic length before and after, $\lambda(w)\lvert dw\rvert=\lambda(z)\lvert dz\rvert$, gives the rule $\lambda$ must obey:

$$\lambda\big(\varphi_a(z)\big)\,\lvert\varphi_a'(z)\rvert=\lambda(z)\qquad\text{for every }a.$$
3

Two facts about $\varphi_a$. Both are short algebra, using only $\lvert w\rvert^2=w\bar w$. The quotient rule gives the derivative; multiplying out $1-\lvert\varphi_a\rvert^2$ gives the second:

$$\lvert\varphi_a'(z)\rvert=\frac{1-\lvert a\rvert^2}{\lvert 1-\bar a z\rvert^2},\qquad 1-\lvert\varphi_a(z)\rvert^2=\frac{(1-\lvert a\rvert^2)(1-\lvert z\rvert^2)}{\lvert 1-\bar a z\rvert^2}.$$
4

Test a candidate. Try $\lambda_0(z)=\dfrac{1}{1-\lvert z\rvert^2}$ in the rule from step 2. Substituting the two facts and cancelling:

$$\lambda_0(\varphi_a(z))\,\lvert\varphi_a'(z)\rvert=\frac{\lvert\varphi_a'(z)\rvert}{1-\lvert\varphi_a(z)\rvert^2}=\frac{\dfrac{1-\lvert a\rvert^2}{\lvert 1-\bar a z\rvert^2}}{\dfrac{(1-\lvert a\rvert^2)(1-\lvert z\rvert^2)}{\lvert 1-\bar a z\rvert^2}}=\frac{1}{1-\lvert z\rvert^2}=\lambda_0(z).$$

It passes. So the length element $\dfrac{\lvert dz\rvert}{1-\lvert z\rvert^2}$ is invariant: it assigns a curve the same length no matter which $\varphi_a$ you view it through. ("Preserved" / "invariant" just means unchanged when you apply a symmetry map.) That is precisely the behaviour an isometry-respecting metric needs.

5

Why it is the only shape, up to one constant. Suppose $\lambda$ is any function obeying the rule. Then $\lambda$ and $\lambda_0$ transform identically, so their ratio $f=\lambda/\lambda_0$ is unchanged by every $\varphi_a$: $f(\varphi_a(z))=f(z)$. But the $\varphi_a$ reach every point (homogeneity: some $\varphi_a$ slides any point to any other), so a function that is constant along them is constant everywhere. Hence $f\equiv k$ and $\lambda=k\,\lambda_0=\dfrac{k}{1-\lvert z\rvert^2}$. Symmetry has fixed the shape; only the number $k$ is left.

6

The constant, from curvature. Re-running the same argument on the disk of radius $1/\sqrt c$ (its defining inequality $c\lVert x\rVert^2<1$ simply replaces $\lvert z\rvert^2<1$) gives $\lambda=\dfrac{k}{1-c\lVert x\rVert^2}$. Feed this to the toolbox. Differentiating $\ln\lambda=\ln k-\ln(1-c\lVert x\rVert^2)$, the constant $\ln k$ dies, leaving $\partial_i\ln\lambda=\dfrac{2c\,x_i}{1-c\lVert x\rVert^2}$; summing the second derivatives,

$$\Delta\ln\lambda=\frac{4c}{(1-c\lVert x\rVert^2)^2},\qquad \lambda^2=\frac{k^2}{(1-c\lVert x\rVert^2)^2},\qquad K=-\frac{1}{\lambda^2}\Delta\ln\lambda=-\frac{4c}{k^2}.$$
∎

We built the space to have curvature $-c$. Setting $-4c/k^2=-c$ gives $k^2=4$, so $k=2$ (positive, since a length scale is positive). That is the entire reason for the 2: it is the unique constant that makes the curvature come out to exactly $-c$. And $\lambda$ grows without bound as $c\lVert x\rVert^2\to1$ (the stretch becomes infinite at the rim), confirming the boundary radius $1/\sqrt c$.

$$\boxed{\;\lambda_x=\dfrac{2}{1-c\lVert x\rVert^2}\;}$$

The flat limit, shown with limits

Sending the curvature to zero should turn this curved geometry back into ordinary flat geometry. Two limits make that exact. First the factor itself — for any fixed point $x$,

$$\lim_{c\to0}\lambda_x=\lim_{c\to0}\frac{2}{1-c\lVert x\rVert^2}=\frac{2}{1-0}=2,$$

so $\lambda$ stops varying from point to point and freezes at the constant $2$. Second the curvature: a constant $\lambda$ has $\ln\lambda$ constant, so $\Delta\ln\lambda=0$ and $K=0$ by the toolbox — flat. A constant conformal factor is just the Euclidean plane with its ruler globally rescaled by $2$, which has zero curvature. (Distances confirm it: the distance law derived in Part IV is $d_c(0,x)=\tfrac{2}{\sqrt c}\tanh^{-1}(\sqrt c\lVert x\rVert)$, and since $\tanh^{-1}t=t+\tfrac{t^3}{3}+\cdots$, the leading term gives $d_c(0,x)\to 2\lVert x\rVert$ as $c\to0$ — Euclidean distance, scaled by the same $2$.)

We now have a concrete place to compute — the Poincaré ball — and its exact ruler, $\lambda_x=2/(1-c\lVert x\rVert^2)$. That is the whole inheritance from history; everything ahead is built on this one metric.

Part IIINetworks — the Euclidean machine, and the one thing that changes
08 What a network is made of

A neural network is five Euclidean operations

A neural network is a function on vectors (points in $\mathbb{R}^n$), built from a small fixed set of operations and trained by sliding its weights downhill against a loss. There are five operations. It translates a vector by adding a bias, $x+b$; mixes coordinates with a weight matrix, $Wx$; bends each coordinate through a nonlinearity $\phi$ (such as $\tanh$); compares two vectors by distance $\lVert x-y\rVert$ or dot product $\langle x,y\rangle$; and classifies by which side of a flat hyperplane a point lands on. A layer chains the first three, $x\mapsto\phi(Wx+b)$; a classifier uses the last two.

The point to notice: every one of these is defined with flat geometry. Addition is ordinary vector addition. "Close" means small $\lVert x-y\rVert$, the straight-line distance. A decision boundary is a flat plane. The network's entire sense of space is the Euclidean dot product — and that single assumption is the one thing a hyperbolic network discards.

09 Where the two architectures differ

The same five operations, rebuilt on the ball

That table is the entire difference between a Euclidean and a hyperbolic network. The architecture is identical — the same layers, the same weights, the same gradient-descent training. What changes is that each operation is replaced by a version respecting the curved metric of the Poincaré ball instead of the flat dot product. The right-hand column is exactly what Part IV derives, line by line; it is the machinery the rest of the document builds.

Why pay that price? Because of the room argument from Part I, which a flat network cannot escape. A Euclidean network can only place its points and draw its boundaries in flat space, where the area within radius $r$ grows polynomially — far too slowly to hold a branching tree, so the tree is crushed: high distortion, and at depth the points collide within floating-point precision. The ball's area grows exponentially, matching the tree. Slide the curvature and watch the gap between the two open up:

Two consequences of "the edge is infinitely stretched" will drive everything: adding points cannot be ordinary vector addition (the sum would shoot out of the disk), and distance is not the straight-line gap. We tackle addition first, because distance is built from it.

12 Adding points

Möbius addition

To "translate" a point $v$ by a point $u$ while staying on the ball, ordinary $u+v$ will not do. The correct operation is Möbius addition, written $\oplus$. First, the term it belongs to:

Gyrovector addition that respects the curve

A gyrovector space is the curved-space counterpart of an ordinary vector space. Möbius addition $\oplus$ plays the role on the ball that plain vector addition $+$ plays in flat space — it is how you translate one point by another without leaving the disk. The prefix gyro warns that $\oplus$ is slightly twisted: it is not quite commutative or associative, and the leftover twist is a built-in rotation called a gyration. Near the centre the twist vanishes and $\oplus$ becomes ordinary $+$.

Derivation — Möbius additionit is the disk's own translation
$$u\oplus_c v=\frac{(1+2c\langle u,v\rangle+c\lVert v\rVert^2)\,u+(1-c\lVert u\rVert^2)\,v}{1+2c\langle u,v\rangle+c^2\lVert u\rVert^2\lVert v\rVert^2}$$
1

In the unit disk seen as complex numbers, the map that slides the disk onto itself, sending the origin to $a$, is the simplest Möbius transformation. Define addition to be that translation:

$$a\oplus b=\frac{a+b}{1+\bar a\,b}.$$
2

Make it real-valued: multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $1+a\bar b$. The denominator becomes $\lvert 1+\bar a b\rvert^2=1+2\langle a,b\rangle+\lVert a\rVert^2\lVert b\rVert^2$ (using $2\langle a,b\rangle=\bar a b+a\bar b$).

∎

Expanding the numerator and using the identity $a^2\bar b=2\langle a,b\rangle a-\lVert a\rVert^2 b$ turns it into exactly the vector form (at $c=1$). General $c$ follows by the rescaling $x\mapsto\sqrt c\,x$, which inserts the $c$'s shown in the target.

For small norms every correction term vanishes and u ⊕ v → u + v — the flat behaviour returns near the centre.

13 Measuring distance

How far apart are two points?

Distance is "length of the shortest path," and length is the metric integrated along that path. For the special case of a path heading straight out from the centre this is a one-dimensional integral we can do by hand — but it uses the hyperbolic functions, so we define those from scratch first.

⊞Toolbox · the three hyperbolic functionsA

These are the hyperbolic analogues of sine and cosine, built directly from the exponential — no triangles needed:

$$\cosh t=\frac{e^{t}+e^{-t}}{2},\quad \sinh t=\frac{e^{t}-e^{-t}}{2},\quad \tanh t=\frac{\sinh t}{\cosh t}.$$

One identity earns its keep repeatedly: $\cosh^2 t-\sinh^2 t=1$. Proof: expand the squares — $\frac{(e^t+e^{-t})^2-(e^t-e^{-t})^2}{4}=\frac{4}{4}=1$. Dividing it by $\cosh^2 t$ gives the form we need below, $1-\tanh^2 t=1/\cosh^2 t$.

⊞Toolbox · the inverse tanh, and the radial distanceB

The inverse of $\tanh$ has a logarithm form, $\tanh^{-1}z=\tfrac12\ln\frac{1+z}{1-z}$ (solve $z=\tanh t$ for $t$), and differentiating it gives $\frac{d}{dz}\tanh^{-1}z=\frac{1}{1-z^2}$.

So the distance straight out from the centre to a point $z$ is the metric integrated radially:

$$d(0,z)=\int_0^{\lVert z\rVert}\frac{2}{1-t^2}\,dt=2\tanh^{-1}\lVert z\rVert.$$

The integrand is the conformal factor $\lambda_t$ at $c=1$; the radial line is the shortest path by symmetry; and $2\tanh^{-1}$ is its antiderivative by the derivative just stated. The shaded area in the figure below is this distance — slide the radius toward the edge and watch it run to infinity.

For two arbitrary points we use a trick we have already earned: Möbius addition by $-u$ is one of the isometries the metric was built to respect, and it slides $u$ to the centre. (Quick check: $-u\oplus u=0$, since both coefficients in the formula become $1-\lVert u\rVert^2$ and the numerator is $(1-\lVert u\rVert^2)(-u+u)=0$.) Because it preserves distance, the gap between $u$ and $v$ equals the gap between the centre and the slid image of $v$:

$$d_c(u,v)=d_c\big(0,\,-u\oplus_c v\big)=\frac{2}{\sqrt c}\tanh^{-1}\!\big(\sqrt c\,\lVert -u\oplus_c v\rVert\big).$$

That is the distance the model actually computes. The paper also records it in a closed two-point form that hides the Möbius addition — equation (4). It is worth deriving in full, because the cancellation that makes it clean is instructive.

Derivation — the closed distance, equation (4)𝔻ⁿ, c = 1
$$d_{\mathbb{D}}(x,y)=\cosh^{-1}\!\left(1+2\,\frac{\lVert x-y\rVert^2}{(1-\lVert x\rVert^2)(1-\lVert y\rVert^2)}\right)$$
1

Start from what we just proved, $d_{\mathbb{D}}(x,y)=2\tanh^{-1}\lVert -x\oplus y\rVert$, and write $a=\lVert -x\oplus y\rVert$. We need $a$ in terms of $x,y$.

2

Squaring the Möbius formula and simplifying (with denominator $D=1+2\langle u,v\rangle+\lVert u\rVert^2\lVert v\rVert^2$) gives two clean identities for any $u,v$; the second's numerator $1-\lVert u\rVert^2-\lVert v\rVert^2+\lVert u\rVert^2\lVert v\rVert^2$ factors perfectly:

$$\lVert u\oplus v\rVert^2=\frac{\lVert u\rVert^2+2\langle u,v\rangle+\lVert v\rVert^2}{D},\qquad 1-\lVert u\oplus v\rVert^2=\frac{(1-\lVert u\rVert^2)(1-\lVert v\rVert^2)}{D}.$$
3

Put $u=-x,\ v=y$. Then $\langle u,v\rangle=-\langle x,y\rangle$, so the first numerator becomes $\lVert x\rVert^2-2\langle x,y\rangle+\lVert y\rVert^2=\lVert x-y\rVert^2$, and both identities share the same $D$. Dividing one by the other, $D$ cancels:

$$\frac{a^2}{1-a^2}=\frac{\lVert x-y\rVert^2}{(1-\lVert x\rVert^2)(1-\lVert y\rVert^2)}.$$
∎

Finally convert the $\tanh$ form to a $\cosh$ form. With $d=2t$ and $\tanh t=a$, the double-angle identity $\cosh 2t=\frac{1+\tanh^2 t}{1-\tanh^2 t}=1+\frac{2a^2}{1-a^2}$ (proved from Toolbox A) gives

$$\cosh d=1+2\,\frac{\lVert x-y\rVert^2}{(1-\lVert x\rVert^2)(1-\lVert y\rVert^2)}\;\Rightarrow\; d=\cosh^{-1}(\cdots).$$
As c → 0, cosh⁻¹(1+ε) ≈ √(2ε), so d → 2‖x−y‖ — the Euclidean distance, up to the constant λ₀ = 2.

14 Crossing between flat and curved

The exponential and logarithmic maps

Nonlinearities and gradients are written for flat space. To use them we need a faithful bridge between the curved ball and the flat tangent space at the origin. The exponential map fires a tangent vector out along a geodesic into the ball; the logarithmic map is its inverse, bringing a ball point back to a flat vector. Both are just the radial distance law from Toolbox B, solved for the endpoint.

Derivation — exp and log at the origingeodesic, solved for its end
$$\exp_0^c(v)=\tanh(\sqrt c\lVert v\rVert)\frac{v}{\sqrt c\lVert v\rVert},\qquad \log_0^c(y)=\frac{1}{\sqrt c}\tanh^{-1}(\sqrt c\lVert y\rVert)\frac{y}{\lVert y\rVert}$$
1

From Toolbox B, a geodesic of arclength $s$ from the centre reaches radius $\rho$ with $s=\tfrac{2}{\sqrt c}\tanh^{-1}(\sqrt c\rho)$, hence $\rho=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt c}\tanh(\tfrac{\sqrt c\,s}{2})$.

2

The arclength $\exp_0$ travels is the Riemannian length of the tangent vector $v$, and at the origin $\lambda_0=2$, so $s=2\lVert v\rVert$. The half cancels the two.

∎

Point the result in the direction $v/\lVert v\rVert$: $\exp_0(v)=\tanh(\sqrt c\lVert v\rVert)\,v/(\sqrt c\lVert v\rVert)$. The log map is its literal inverse. The $\tanh$ is a squeeze: however long $v$ is, its image stays inside the ball.

As c → 0, exp₀(v) → v and log₀(y) → y — tangent space and ball merge into one flat space.

15 Multiplying by a matrix

Möbius matrix–vector product

A weight matrix acts linearly — a flat-space operation. So we apply it the only sensible way: drop the point to the flat tangent space with $\log_0$, multiply there, and lift back with $\exp_0$. The closed form is pure substitution.

$$M\otimes_c x:=\exp_0^c\!\big(M\,\log_0^c(x)\big)=\frac{1}{\sqrt c}\tanh\!\Big(\frac{\lVert Mx\rVert}{\lVert x\rVert}\tanh^{-1}(\sqrt c\lVert x\rVert)\Big)\frac{Mx}{\lVert Mx\rVert}.$$

The same recipe with the matrix replaced by an element-wise multiply handles the "gates" the GRU will need, so no separate derivation is required there. As $c\to 0$ both $\tanh$'s linearise and $M\otimes x\to Mx$.

16 Angles come for free

Why hyperbolic angles are Euclidean angles

One more fact closes the toolkit: the angle between two tangent vectors, measured with the curved metric, equals the ordinary angle. This is equation (5), and it is a one-line cancellation — but we first earn the cosine formula it uses.

⊞Toolbox · the cosine of an angleC

For two nonzero vectors, $\cos\theta=\dfrac{\langle u,v\rangle}{\lVert u\rVert\,\lVert v\rVert}$.

Proof from the law of cosines, $\lVert u-v\rVert^2=\lVert u\rVert^2+\lVert v\rVert^2-2\lVert u\rVert\lVert v\rVert\cos\theta$. The dot product expands the same left side as $\lVert u\rVert^2-2\langle u,v\rangle+\lVert v\rVert^2$. Equate, cancel $\lVert u\rVert^2+\lVert v\rVert^2$, and solve.

Derivation — conformal angle, equation (5)the λ cancels
$$\cos\angle(u,v)=\frac{g_x^{\mathbb{D}}(u,v)}{\sqrt{g_x^{\mathbb{D}}(u,u)}\,\sqrt{g_x^{\mathbb{D}}(v,v)}}=\frac{\langle u,v\rangle}{\lVert u\rVert\,\lVert v\rVert}$$
1

The metric is the dot product times one positive number, $g_x(u,v)=\lambda_x^2\langle u,v\rangle$. Drop it into the cosine formula of Toolbox C.

∎

Every term carries the same $\lambda_x^2$; each square root pulls out one $\lambda_x$, so the denominator is $\lambda_x^2\lVert u\rVert\lVert v\rVert$. The $\lambda_x^2$ cancels top and bottom, leaving the bare Euclidean angle — identical at every point of the ball. This is exactly what "conformal" promised back in Part II.

That completes the toolkit: add ($\oplus$), multiply ($\otimes$), cross to flat space and back ($\log_0,\exp_0$), measure distance ($d_c$), and trust angles. Part V wires them into a network.

Part VThe Network — assembling the layers
17 The recurrent cell

The hyperbolic RNN

Recall the flat recurrent update, $h_t=\phi(W h_{t-1}+U x_t+b)$. We rebuild it by translating each piece into its curved twin from Part IV: matrix multiply becomes $\otimes_c$, addition becomes $\oplus_c$, and the nonlinearity is applied through the tangent space (log down, bend, exp back):

$$\tilde h_t=(W\otimes_c h_{t-1})\oplus_c(U\otimes_c x_t)\oplus_c b,\qquad h_t=\exp_0^c\!\big(\phi(\log_0^c \tilde h_t)\big).$$

Nothing here is new — it is the flat cell with every operation swapped for the one we derived. The one-step data flow, exactly as the code runs it, reads left to right:

A subtlety the paper stresses: a stack of hyperbolic matrix-products and nonlinearities with no bias would secretly collapse — the whole stack equals $\exp_0\!\circ f_k\circ\cdots\circ f_1\circ\log_0$, an ordinary Euclidean network wearing a log/exp disguise. What genuinely makes the model hyperbolic is the Möbius bias $\oplus_c b$ slipped between layers: it shifts the point along a geodesic and cannot be undone by that tangent-space trick. (Formally that bias is the parallel transport of a tangent vector to the point — eq. 28 of the paper.)
18 The gated cell

The hyperbolic GRU

The GRU adds two gates — an update gate $z_t$ and a reset gate $r_t$ — each a number in $[0,1]$ per coordinate, computed by the same Möbius-affine-then-$\log_0$-then-$\sigma$ pattern ($\sigma$ is the logistic squashing function). The reset gate multiplies the old state element-wise (the gated Möbius product), feeding a candidate state $\tilde h_t$. The final update is a hyperbolic interpolation walking from the old state toward the candidate:

$$h_t=h_{t-1}\oplus_c\big(z_t\odot_c(-h_{t-1}\oplus_c\tilde h_t)\big).$$

Read the right-hand side as: take the gyro-difference between where you are ($h_{t-1}$) and where you want to be ($\tilde h_t$), scale it by the gate, and Möbius-add it back — the curved version of the flat convex blend $h_{t-1}+z(\tilde h-h_{t-1})$.

19 From sequences to a verdict

Combining two sentences

Each encoder runs its cell across a sentence and outputs the final hidden point — $s_1$ for the first sentence, $s_2$ for the second, both points on the ball. A feed-forward layer then fuses them, and crucially it is also handed their hyperbolic distance $d_c(s_1,s_2)^2$ as an extra feature — the very quantity we derived in Part IV, now doing real work: "how far apart in meaning are these two sentences?" The fused vector is what the classifier sees.

20 The curved classifier

Hyperbolic multinomial logistic regression

Flat logistic regression scores a point by its signed distance to a flat hyperplane. The curved version replaces the plane with a hypergyroplane — a surface anchored at a ball point $p_k$ with a tangent normal direction $a_k$ — and the score is the signed hyperbolic distance to it. That distance turns out to be an inverse-hyperbolic-sine.

⊞Toolbox · the inverse sinhD

$\sinh^{-1}z=\ln(z+\sqrt{z^2+1})$, the inverse of $\sinh$ from Toolbox A. We also use $\sinh(2t)=2\sinh t\cosh t$, immediate from the definitions.

Derivation — the hyperbolic MLR score, eq. (25)distance to a hypergyroplane
$$\ell_k(x)=\frac{\lambda^c_{p_k}\lVert a_k\rVert}{\sqrt c}\,\sinh^{-1}\!\Big(\sqrt c\,\lambda^c_{v_k}\big\langle v_k,\tfrac{a_k}{\lVert a_k\rVert}\big\rangle\Big),\quad v_k=-p_k\oplus_c x$$
1

Translate the anchor to the centre with the isometry from Part IV: set $v=-p_k\oplus_c x$. The hypergyroplane through $p_k$ with normal $a_k$ becomes the flat linear plane through the origin, $\{w:\langle w,a_k\rangle=0\}$.

2

Solve the symmetric case: put $v=t\,\hat a$ on the normal axis. Its distance to that plane is the radial distance $\tfrac{2}{\sqrt c}\tanh^{-1}(\sqrt c\,t)$, and $\sinh$ of it is, by Toolbox D, $\sinh(\sqrt c\,d)=\dfrac{2\sqrt c\,t}{1-c t^2}=\sqrt c\,\lambda_v^c\,t$.

∎

Since $t=\langle v,\hat a\rangle$ and rotating within the plane changes nothing, this holds for all $v$: the distance to the hyperplane is $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt c}\sinh^{-1}(\sqrt c\,\lambda_v^c\langle v,\hat a\rangle)$. The class logit is this signed distance times the metric length of the normal at the anchor, $\sqrt{g^c_{p_k}(a_k,a_k)}=\lambda^c_{p_k}\lVert a_k\rVert$ — the curved echo of how flat MLR weights its distance-to-hyperplane by $\lVert a_k\rVert$.

As c → 0, λ → 2 and the whole logit collapses to 4⟨−pₖ + x, aₖ⟩ — the ordinary Euclidean softmax score (the 4 is (λ⁰)²).

21 Measuring error

The loss

The classifier's $K$ scores $\ell_k$ are exponentiated and normalised into class probabilities (the softmax of §20), and the training objective is ordinary cross-entropy — the standard penalty for putting low probability on the correct label:

$$\mathcal{L}=-\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\log p\big(y=y_i\mid x_i\big).$$

There is no special "hyperbolic loss." The geometry has already done its work upstream: the squared hyperbolic distance $d_c(s_1,s_2)^2$ entered as a feature of the combine layer (§19), so "how far apart in meaning are these sentences" is something the classifier reads, not something the loss bolts on afterward.

22 Training on the ball

Riemannian gradient descent

Training nudges weights downhill — but a naïve Euclidean step on a ball point would be the wrong size and could leave the disk entirely. The fix is one line of the same metric: the proper (Riemannian) gradient is the flat gradient rescaled by the inverse metric factor, after which a step is taken along a geodesic with $\exp_x$.

Derivation — the gradient rescalingone line from the metric
$$\nabla_R f=\frac{1}{(\lambda_x^c)^2}\,\nabla_E f=\frac{(1-c\lVert x\rVert^2)^2}{4}\,\nabla_E f$$
1

The Riemannian gradient is defined so that its metric inner product with any direction equals the directional derivative: $g_x(\nabla_R f,w)=\langle\nabla_E f,w\rangle$ for all $w$.

∎

Substitute $g_x=\lambda_x^2\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$: then $\lambda_x^2\langle\nabla_R f,w\rangle=\langle\nabla_E f,w\rangle$ for all $w$, so $\nabla_R f=\nabla_E f/\lambda_x^2$. That factor is precisely the $1/(\lambda_x^c)^2$ from Part II, which shrinks steps to zero at the boundary — so a parameter can never escape the ball. The update is $x_{t+1}=\exp_{x_t}^c(-\eta\,\nabla_R f)$.

In practice only the genuinely hyperbolic parameters — the word embeddings and the layer biases — are trained this way. The weight matrices live in flat tangent space and use a standard optimiser (Adam); back-propagation runs as usual, with the single Riemannian rescaling applied just to the hyperbolic parameters' gradients.

Part VISynthesis — the whole machine
23 One pass, end to end

Watch a single example flow through

Every piece now exists and is derived. Trace one labelled sentence pair from input to loss, and the document folds shut on itself:

1 · Embed. Each word becomes a vector and is placed on the ball with $\exp_0$ (Part IV §14).

2 · Encode. A hyperbolic RNN or GRU (§17–18) reads each sentence, its hidden state moving on the ball via $\otimes_c$, $\oplus_c$, and a tangent-space nonlinearity, emerging as a single point $s_1$ or $s_2$.

3 · Combine. A feed-forward layer fuses $s_1,s_2$ with their hyperbolic distance $d_c(s_1,s_2)^2$ (§13, §19) — the geometry of meaning entering as a feature.

4 · Classify. Hyperbolic MLR (§20) scores the fused point against each class's curved boundary, giving probabilities.

5 · Score and learn. Cross-entropy (§21) measures the error; Riemannian SGD (§22) pushes every weight — flat ones with ordinary steps, ball-bound ones along geodesics — a little downhill. Repeat.

Every operation in that trace reduces to the single metric of Part II, which itself follows from one changed axiom of Part I — the entire network rests on that chain.
24 Why it was worth it

The payoff, restated

The reason for all this machinery was the exponential room of Part III: hierarchy embeds on the ball with far less distortion than in the same number of flat dimensions, because the space grows as fast as the data branches. Hyperbolic encoders place broad concepts near the centre and specific ones out toward the boundary — encoding the tree in the radius — which is exactly the structure the curved distance and curved classifier are built to read.

Negate one axiom about parallel lines, and you are handed a curvature, a unit of length, a single metric — and a neural network that thinks in the shape of a tree. Everything else is consequence.
